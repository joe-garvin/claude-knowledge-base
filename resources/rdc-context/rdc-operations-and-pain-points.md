# RDC operations and pain points

## How work flows

RDC uses a structured but flexible creative workflow. The general shape:

1. **Kickoff / discovery** — Meeting with client stakeholders to clarify objectives, audience, scope, and success metrics. Naj or a PM owns this phase.
2. **Planning** — Milestones, schedules, and roles are drafted. Content and design begin working from briefs or past examples.
3. **Production** — Cross-functional teams (writers, designers, motion, video, PM) execute in parallel or in sequence depending on the deliverable type.
4. **Review / iteration** — Iterative feedback loops with client stakeholders built into the process. Regular check-ins and shared project dashboards.
5. **Quality assurance** — Peer review, SME validation, brand guideline checks, accessibility checks.
6. **Delivery and handoff** — Final deliverables delivered; documentation and handoff materials provided.

## Tools and platforms in use

**Project management:** monday.com (primary project tracker for task tracking, status, and assignments)

**Communication and collaboration:** Microsoft Teams (internal and client-facing), SharePoint (document storage and sharing), email (Outlook/M365). Per the employee handbook, RDC maintains an "open door" communication policy and prefers direct communication via email, phone, or Teams. Benefits and HR are administered via Gusto.

**Remote-first workplace:** RDC is fully remote/virtual. All team members work from home offices. The handbook outlines expectations around data security, confidentiality, and professional conduct for remote employees.

**File storage (distributed — see pain points):**
- SharePoint (multiple sites — see below)
- Box (RDC Box account)
- Google Drive
- Individual hard drives / local storage

**Video review and collaboration:** Frame.io

**Design and production:** Adobe Creative Suite (Illustrator, Photoshop, After Effects, Premiere, etc.), PowerPoint, Figma (likely)

**Writing and content:** Microsoft Word, Google Docs, SharePoint

**Client-embedded tools:** Embedded consultants at Microsoft use Microsoft's internal tools and environments (Teams, SharePoint, internal systems)

## Known operational pain points

### Fragmented file storage
Files live across Box, SharePoint (multiple sites), Google Drive, and local drives. There is no single source of truth for assets, past deliverables, or reference materials. Finding a past project or brand asset requires knowing which platform and which folder it was stored in — and this knowledge often lives with individuals rather than being documented or searchable across the organization. This creates:
- Duplicated effort (re-creating things that already exist)
- Inconsistency (using outdated versions or off-brand assets)
- Onboarding friction (new team members don't know where things live)
- Time lost to file hunting

### Siloed AI experimentation
Some team members use Claude regularly and have developed effective workflows. Others have used it occasionally or not at all. The experimentation and learning that has happened is siloed — people don't know what others have tried, what worked, or what failed. There's no shared knowledge base, template library, or documented AI workflow. This means:
- The same problems get solved individually by different people
- Effective prompts and workflows aren't shared or standardized
- New team members can't benefit from what others have learned
- RDC can't assess its own AI maturity or set direction as an organization

### Lack of cross-team visibility into workflows and pain points
People don't have a clear view into how work flows through other disciplines (e.g., how writers hand off to designers, how PMs track capacity, how embedded consultants differ from in-house team). This makes it harder to:
- Identify opportunities for process improvement
- Brainstorm how to share tools or shortcuts across roles
- Anticipate bottlenecks before they happen
- Build org-wide AI or workflow initiatives

### Distributed team context (embedded vs. in-house)
The embedded consultants at Microsoft work in a completely different environment from the in-house creative team — different tools, different client relationships, different rhythms. Their context, constraints, and opportunities rarely make it back to the broader team.

### Time as the primary constraint
The team is small and the deliverable volume is high. Most internal work involves scoping client projects, building deliverables from past examples, drafting content to a known structure, and coordinating across stakeholders. The biggest lever across all roles is reducing the time it takes to do recurring, structured work.

### AI maturity is mixed
Some team members are confident Claude users. Others have never used it or have used it once or twice with unclear results. There is no shared onboarding path, no internal documentation of what works, and no regular touchpoint where the team shares what they're learning. The RDC AI Discovery skill was developed to help surface individual-level opportunities, but there's no org-level counterpart.

### Scale and coordination across two distinct operational contexts
The org is larger than it may appear from a Creative Team vantage point. The Azure embedded team alone has nine people working inside Microsoft. The VSS account team has five more. These client-embedded teams operate in a fundamentally different environment from the in-house Creative Team. Knowledge, tools, and workflows rarely cross between the two contexts — compounding the siloed learning problem.

## Strengths of current operations

- Strong cross-functional team with clear discipline leads (content, visual, motion)
- PMs provide good liaison and coordination structure
- Collaborative culture; team is proactive about feedback and quality
- Enterprise-level experience means the team is accustomed to rigorous client standards
- Cloud-based collaboration tools (Teams, SharePoint, Frame.io) support remote and cross-functional work
- Structured but flexible workflow allows adaptation to changing client needs
