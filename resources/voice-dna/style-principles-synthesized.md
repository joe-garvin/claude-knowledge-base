# Style principles — synthesized

Distilled from the style principles log. Updated periodically as the log grows. Intended for injection into future drafting prompts to improve first-draft quality.

**Last updated:** 2026-04-28

## Specificity and detail

- Preserve concrete details: numbers, names, sensory observations, quoted speech. These are load-bearing, not decorative.
- Parentheticals and asides often carry the most authentic voice. Don't cut them for smoothness.
- If a detail was in the source material, there's usually a reason. Flag before removing, don't silently compress.

## Closings and resolution

- In personal correspondence, slightly unresolved or uncertain closings read as more human than tidy composed ones.
- Don't tie the bow too tight. Let the ending reflect where the writer actually is.

## Register and voice

- When a writer's register includes self-aware phrases ("I guess," "thanks for letting me share this"), preserve them. They're relational signals, not filler.
- Smoothing ≠ improving. A cleaner sentence is not always a truer one.
- Warm, relationally-grounded openers land better than functional ones in personal correspondence. Surface gratitude or connection early.

## General drafting posture

- Prioritize fidelity to the source material's texture over editorial polish.
- When in doubt between two versions, ask: which one sounds more like a person wrote it in a specific moment?
