## 2026-04-28 — Personal email to Nomon (memorial service update)

**Context:** Personal correspondence to Zen guiding teacher. Conversational register, warm and deferential. Subject matter: update on brother Marty's memorial service.

**Principles extracted:**

- **Restore specificity before polishing rhythm.** Numbers, names, sensory details, and quoted speech are load-bearing. Don't compress them in the name of flow. (Claude cut: headcount, geography of attendees, sensory detail of incense and kneeling, physical description of priest, holy water moment, full name of liturgical element.)

- **Parentheticals and asides are often where the real voice lives.** Flag them before cutting. (Claude removed the parenthetical examples of emotions — "relief, hope, rage, irritation" — and the pointed observation about grieving-process expectations. These were doing real work.)

- **Honest, slightly unresolved closings are stronger than composed ones in personal correspondence.** Don't tie the bow too tight. (Claude's version: "I'm still sitting with all of it. If anything comes up for you in reading this, I'd welcome it." — sounds literary. Revised version acknowledged uncertainty and messiness directly: "I guess I have no specific requests... I'm just sitting with all this as I return to Bellingham now, and I felt like sharing with you.")

- **When the writer's register includes self-awareness about the letter itself, preserve it.** Phrases like "I guess," "thanks for letting me share this lengthy report" are part of the relationship, not throat-clearing. Smoothing them out flattens the voice.

- **Smoothing a draft can inadvertently flatten it.** The goal is clarity, not polish. A cleaner sentence is not always a truer one.

- **Warm openers that acknowledge the relationship land better than functional ones.** Claude opened transactionally ("You mentioned you were looking forward..."). Revised version surfaced gratitude earlier and felt more like how a person actually starts a personal letter.
