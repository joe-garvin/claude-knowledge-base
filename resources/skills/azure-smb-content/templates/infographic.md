# Infographic template

## Format principles

- Primary audience: BDMs — business outcomes, not technical specs
- Answer "How does this help my business?" and "How do I start safely?"
- Show what's possible today; avoid long-term transformation framing
- Real-world use cases and success stories outperform abstract capability descriptions
- Demystify AI — make it feel achievable, not overwhelming
- Highly scannable: a busy BDM should grasp the gist in under 60 seconds

---

## Use case structure

Every use case follows four beats. Same thinking across all use cases — but not the same phrasing pattern.

**1. Business problem**
Plain language, the way a business owner would say it. Creates "that's us" recognition. Name the pain immediately — don't walk the reader through it. If the first sentence requires two or three sentences of setup before it reaches the actual problem, the setup is doing work the reader doesn't need done for them.

**2. What changed**
How AI helped, in terms of what it does. Mention Azure/Microsoft once, naturally, where it fits the sentence — as a trusted enabler, not a product feature list. Don't insert a nearly identical Azure sentence in the same structural position in every section. If the Azure mention reads the same way across consecutive sections, it reads as a template insertion rather than an integrated claim.

**3. Business outcome**
Specific, tangible result. Numbers when available. Always in business terms (time, money, satisfaction, error reduction) — never technical terms (latency, throughput, model accuracy). The lead-in to the proof point ("For one [descriptor]...") should vary in syntax and emphasis across use cases so it doesn't read as a repeated formula.

**4. Inclusive closer**
1–2 sentences bridging the specific story to the reader's situation. Makes it feel achievable for a business of any size. Vary the length, syntax, and emphasis of closing sentences across sections — when every use case ends with the same rhetorical move, the repetition becomes audible by the second one.

## Use case evaluation checklist

Apply to every use case received from a client or PMM. All four tests must pass for a use case to earn a place in the infographic.

1. **BDM recognition:** Does the underlying business problem resonate with a business owner? Would they recognize it immediately? Flag if the problem is too technical, internal, or obscure.
2. **Achievability:** Is the solution achievable without a big IT lift or 6-month implementation? Flag if it requires a dedicated AI team or complex infrastructure.
3. **Business-language outcome:** Is the result measurable in business terms — time, money, customer satisfaction, error reduction — without needing technical metrics? Flag if the outcome is only describable in technical terms.
4. **Size inclusivity:** Could a 10-person business and a 300-person business both see themselves in this story? Flag if it only makes sense for a specific size, industry, or technical maturity level.

## High-intent use case categories (from the MPF)

Microsoft's MPF identifies these six SMB AI use case categories as highest-intent. Use these to inform framing and grouping when use cases arrive from a client.

1. Automate everyday operations — free up your team from repetitive tasks (data entry, scheduling, document management, dashboards, forecasting)
2. Smarter customer support — handle complex customer questions automatically so your team focuses on relationships (AI assistants, ticket routing, real-time support)
3. Personalize customer experiences — make every interaction feel tailored without a large team to do it manually (product recommendations, brand voice, loyalty, e-commerce)
4. Data-driven marketing and insights — turn business data into content and decisions without a dedicated analyst (marketing copy, audience targeting, A/B testing)
5. Empower your team with internal AI tools — give every employee access to AI that helps them do more in less time (meeting summaries, email drafting, task automation, knowledge search)
6. Policy and document management — reduce the overhead of compliance, paperwork, and policy reviews without a legal department (compliance checks, document workflows, gap detection)

## Anti-patterns

Derived from revision analysis on the SMB AI use cases infographic project.

### Formulaic section structure
Each use case should follow the same thinking — problem, solution, proof, bridge — but not the same phrasing pattern. When every section ends with the same rhetorical move, the repetition becomes audible by the second use case. Vary the length, syntax, and emphasis of closing sentences across sections even when the underlying message is similar.

### Boilerplate Azure mentions
Mention Azure or Microsoft once per use case, naturally, where it fits the sentence. Don't insert a nearly identical Azure sentence in the same structural position in every section. If the Azure mention reads the same way across consecutive sections, it reads as a template insertion rather than a credential.

### Narrative setup before the problem
Don't walk the reader through the business problem — let them recognize it. The opening of each use case should name the pain in terms the reader already knows, not explain it to someone encountering it for the first time.

### Soundbite sentences
Avoid sentences designed to land as quotable observations. In plain-language SMB copy, these call attention to themselves and undercut the trusted-peer tone. If a sentence sounds like it belongs in a conference keynote or on a pull quote card, rewrite it as a plain statement of fact.

Examples to avoid: "Fraud doesn't announce itself." / "The advantage AI brings isn't just speed — it's coverage."

### Contrastive negation
Don't use "it's not X, it's Y" or "not just X, it's Y" constructions. These read as AI-generated and feel argumentative rather than confident. Make the positive claim directly.

Instead of: "Better customer service doesn't always mean more staff."
Write: "AI handles the volume so your team focuses on the work that actually needs them."

### Weak parenthetical elaborations
Avoid mid-sentence parentheticals that list examples the reader didn't need listed. "— files, emails, records —" style inserts usually signal that the preceding noun wasn't specific enough, or that the writer is padding for confidence. Either be more specific in the main clause, or cut the elaboration.

### Trailing clauses that don't add meaning
Watch the end of closing sentences. Drafts tend to append a phrase that restates what the sentence already said. If the last clause of a closing sentence could be removed without losing meaning, remove it.

### Transitional throat-clearing
Avoid sentences that announce what the next sentence will say rather than just saying it. "A real-world example with one auto dealership platform shows what this shift looks like in practice" does no work that "CallRevu is an auto dealership platform that..." doesn't also do, more efficiently. Cut the announcement and start the example.

### Word and phrase repetition across use cases
Scan across all use cases before finalizing. Watch for:
- The same transition formula appearing more than once in proof point openers ("For one [descriptor], the results were measurable")
- High-frequency words repeated within a single use case ("focus on" appearing twice in consecutive paragraphs)
- Rhetorical devices used for emphasis that also appear in a nearby section (e.g., anaphora in a closing line that mirrors an opener in the same use case)
- The headline verb echoed in the first sentence of the intro copy
