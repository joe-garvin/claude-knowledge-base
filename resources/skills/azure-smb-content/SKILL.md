# Azure SMB content — language and messaging principles

This skill encodes the SMB-specific language principles, tone guidelines, copy conventions, and messaging frameworks from Microsoft's SMB Integrated Marketing team (primarily Stephanie Van Kol) and the official FY26 H1 Messaging & Positioning Framework (MPF). Apply these whenever writing Azure content targeting small and medium business audiences.

---

## Part 1: SMB audience segments

Microsoft's SMB 3x3 framework defines three segments, each requiring a different content approach:

**Classic SMB** — the primary target for most RDC SMB content
- Regionally focused businesses with low or no in-house IT staff
- Under ~500 employees; strongly identify with "SMB" terminology
- Low technical maturity; decisions are business-led, often by the owner or a manager wearing many hats
- Seek immediate, practical solutions — not long-term strategic roadmaps
- Top concerns: cost predictability, ease of use, security, and fast time to value
- Messaging approach: lead with foundational business outcomes, plain language, remove technical terminology

**Tech Engaged**
- Strong in-house technical skills; often ISVs, software companies, or managed IT providers
- More comfortable with technical language; "innovate" framing can work for this audience
- Messaging approach: can leverage SDC/developer language; still include SMB customer stories

**Very Large SMBs (formerly Large Unmanaged)**
- Larger companies that don't meet Microsoft's enterprise account management thresholds
- Profiles are closer to enterprise; often use other cloud providers
- Messaging approach: leverage enterprise/solution play narratives without modification

**Default assumption**: unless a brief specifies otherwise, write for Classic SMB.

---

## Part 2: The SMB mindset

Classic SMB buyers are practical, resource-conscious, and directly accountable for results. They are not planning multi-year digital transformation roadmaps. They want to know: "How does this help my business right now, and can I afford it?"

Key emotional reality: SMBs are AI-curious but cautious. They are aware of hype, uncertain about costs and complexity, and worried about making the wrong investment. Content should feel like a trusted guide, not a pitch.

Key decision drivers (from research):
- Cost predictability and clear ROI
- Ease of use — no need for specialized staff
- Security and data protection
- Speed to value — fast setup, immediate results
- Trust — proven technology used by businesses like theirs

Top SMB anxieties to address or neutralize in content:
- "This is too complex for a business our size"
- "We can't afford the IT staff to manage this"
- "We're not sure it will actually work for us"
- "We don't want to be locked in or surprised by costs"

## Part 3: Language rules

### Words and phrases to use

| Use this | Instead of |
|----------|------------|
| Business / company | Organization |
| Technology / technology setup / existing systems | IT estate / IT infrastructure |
| Growing businesses / businesses of all sizes | SMB (in titles and headlines) |
| Medium | Midsize |
| Modernize your systems / upgrade and simplify | Digital transformation |
| At your own pace / ready when you are | [urgency-first language] |
| Businesses of all sizes | [size-exclusive language] |
| Practical / right away / immediate | [abstract or long-horizon language] |
| Enterprise-grade security (when needed) | Enterprise (as general audience framing) |
| Grow / save time / stay competitive | Innovate / Innovation (for Classic SMB) |

### Words and phrases to avoid

- **"Organization"** — too formal, too enterprise; use "business" or "company"
- **"IT estate"** or **"data estate"** — enterprise jargon; use "technology setup," "existing systems," or "current technology"
- **"Enterprise"** — use only when describing an attribute (e.g., "enterprise-grade security"), never as an audience or aspiration frame
- **"Frontier Firm"** — does not resonate with Classic SMB; if used, must include a brief explainer
- **"Innovation" / "Innovate"** — too abstract for Classic SMB; tie to tangible outcomes (time saved, cost reduced, customers served) or replace with "grow," "improve," or "compete"
- **"Digital transformation"** — implies long-term planning; Classic SMBs don't think this way; use "modernize your systems," "upgrade," or "simplify"
- **"SMB" in titles or headlines** — businesses over 500 employees may not identify with the term; use "growing businesses," "businesses of all sizes," or "business growth" in titles; "SMB" can appear within body copy for Classic SMB or Azure Conversation-level content
- **"AI"** as a standalone concept — must be tied to tangible outcomes (automation, time savings, cost reduction, simplicity); do not lead with AI capabilities alone
- **Long-term roadmap language** — "strategic roadmap," "multi-year plan," "journey to X" implies planning horizons Classic SMBs don't have
- **Overly casual or consumer-y tone** — SMBs are businesses; content should feel professional and credible, not startup-casual

### Inclusive sizing language

- For content targeting all SMB sizes (25–999 employees): use "growing businesses," "businesses of all sizes," "business growth"
- For Classic SMB specifically (under ~500 employees): "SMB" is acceptable within body copy and introductions
- Upper medium SMBs (500+ employees): frame as "growing businesses," not "SMB"
- Preferred size term: "medium" (not "midsize")

## Part 4: Tone and voice

**Lead with benefits, not products or features.** Start with what the business gains, then explain how. Classic SMBs don't care what the product is called — they care what it does for them.

**Emphasize flexibility and ease.** SMBs need to know they can adopt at their own pace, scale as they grow, and get started without hiring specialists. Phrases like "at your own pace," "scales with your business," and "no need for a dedicated IT team" directly address real anxieties.

**Focus on practical, immediate value.** "What can this do for my business today and tomorrow?" is the SMB question. Content should answer it directly and quickly. Avoid long-horizon or transformation-first framing.

**Plain language throughout.** Even when covering technical topics, write for a BDM who is not an IT specialist. Demystify — do not impress. Analogies are useful.

**Personable and authentic.** Avoid overly polished, cinematic enterprise AI promo energy. SMBs should feel like this technology is for them, not intimidating or out of reach.

**Confidence-building.** Proof points, customer stories, and concrete outcomes build trust. Reference businesses similar in size and context where possible.

## Part 5: Messaging pillars and hero statements

### SMB hero statement (short)
"Bring Azure AI to your business: Build and run AI apps and agents on Microsoft Azure that are secure, cost-effective, and adaptable to your needs. Get future-proof technology, with ease and predictability."

### SMB expanded positioning
"Grow your business with Microsoft Azure: Easily create and manage AI apps and agents on a complete platform that adapts to your needs — helping you empower your teams to do more, reduce repetition-induced cost centers, and bring delight to your customers. With built-in security and cost controls, you get the latest AI tools, without needing to expand the IT department."

### Four messaging pillars (FY26 H1)

| Pillar | Tagline | SMB angle |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Build AI solutions faster with fewer developers | "Build AI with AI" | Lean teams can do more with GitHub Copilot and AI-assisted tools |
| Orchestrate AI systems on a unified platform | "Orchestrate at ease" | One place to connect models, data, and agents — no manual stitching |
| Scale when ready with confidence | "Scale as needed" | Start small, grow without re-architecting; no dedicated ops team needed |
| Secure, govern, and ensure safety of your AI | "Secure by default" | Built-in protections; no security specialist required |

### Problems Azure solves for SMBs (copy-ready framing)
- Limited budgets and IT resources make advanced technology hard to access → affordable, pay-as-you-go pricing
- Security and data protection are constant worries → automatic updates and built-in fraud protection
- Fragmented tools and complex updates slow progress → all-in-one platform, everything works together
- Staying ahead of competitors and keeping customers engaged → proven tools used by businesses worldwide, now tailored for SMBs

## Part 6: Content-type-specific guidance

### E-book titles
- Do not use "SMB" in the title for content targeting businesses 500+ employees
- Use inclusive language: "growing businesses," "businesses of all sizes," "business growth"
- Classic SMB content: introduce SMB references in the introduction, not the title
- Lead with the business outcome, not the product or technology

**Title transformation examples:**

| Enterprise title | SMB title |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Secure the shift: Navigating security challenges in migration and modernization | Cloud Security Essentials: 5 best practices for migration and modernization |
| Migrating and Modernizing your IT estate: Cloud transformation in Azure | Cloud Migration and Modernization for Growing Businesses: Streamline, automate and reduce costs with Azure |
| Secure and Govern Your Data with Microsoft Fabric and Microsoft Purview | Securing Business Data: A guide to protecting and simplifying your data strategy |
| Intelligent App Roadmap: Build and Modernize Intelligent Apps | Roadmap to Intelligent Apps: Practical guidance for leaders seeking to modernize their apps |

### Social cards and short-form copy
Two-part structure that works well:
1. Short benefit-led statement (4–7 words) — outcome or benefit, not process
2. Supporting line that expands with specifics or a call to action

Keep language direct and declarative. Lead with aspiration over urgency.

### Infographics
- Primary audience: BDMs — business outcomes, not technical specs
- Answer "How does this help my business?" and "How do I start safely?"
- Show what's possible today and tomorrow; avoid long-term transformation framing
- Real-world use cases and success stories outperform abstract capability descriptions
- Demystify AI — make it feel achievable, not overwhelming

### CTAs by segment

**Classic SMB**
- Primary: Find a Partner (Migrate and Innovate plays) / Find a partner (Unify play)
- Secondary: Azure Accelerate
- Tertiary: Get Started with Azure (Migrate/Innovate) / Get started with Fabric (Unify)
- When mentioning Azure Accelerate, consider integrating Cloud Accelerate Factory to emphasize zero-cost deployment

**Tech Engaged**
- Primary: Get Started with Azure
- Secondary: Azure Migrate
- Tertiary: Azure Accelerate or Hybrid Benefit page

### Photography (when providing image direction)
- Favor people over graphics or illustrations
- Show people working and appearing happy — not stressed, not in formal suits or conference rooms
- Mix of office and industry environments; do not skew toward tiny startup aesthetics (1–25 employee look)
- Balanced demographic representation; welcoming and professional
- Avoid heavily edited or AI-generated-looking images of people
- Avoid cliché solo-posed hero images (acceptable in e-book interiors)

## Part 7: Customer stories guidance

- Prioritize SMB customer stories when customizing or supporting content
- Select stories published within the past two years
- Filter by: Azure + relevant product(s); organization size 50–999 employees; partners if applicable
- Stories from businesses similar in size and industry to the target audience build the most trust

## Part 9: Behavior rules

- Always identify which SMB segment is being targeted before writing; default to Classic SMB if unspecified
- Lead with business outcomes, not product names or feature lists
- Apply language rules consistently — flag enterprise terminology in source materials before drafting
- When a brief contains enterprise-first positioning, adapt it proactively rather than copying it through
- If the target audience segment is ambiguous, ask before writing
- Do not use "SMB" in titles unless the brief explicitly calls for it; use inclusive alternatives
