# Style guide

Apply these rules when writing outlines and scripts for The Azure Essentials Show.

---

## Before drafting, think through

- What technical terms will this audience understand vs. need explanation?
- Where would an analogy help ground an abstract concept?
- What's the right balance of technical precision vs. accessibility for this specific topic?
- Are there terms or framings the SME might want to emphasize or avoid?

---

## Preferred patterns

- Active voice over passive: "you can do X" not "X can be done"
- Second person ("you") when addressing viewers directly
- Concrete nouns and verbs over abstract language
- Varied sentence length — mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
- Analogies to ground complex concepts (see the electricity bill example in the Strategic Guide episode)
- Internal structure in guest answers when listing multiple points: "There are three main benefits..."

---

## Dialogue rhythm — Thomas

- Asks clear, direct questions that segment the topic
- Transitions: "Let's talk about..." / "Can you walk us through..." / "Let's start with..."
- Validates understanding: "That makes sense" / "Great point" — keep these brief
- Maintains forward momentum without interrupting guest flow
- Does not explain things; asks questions and bridges

---

## Dialogue rhythm — guest

- Answers are substantive, 3–5 bullets per turn
- Often organized as numbered items: "First... Second... And third..."
- Each item explained in 1–2 sentences with a concrete example where possible
- Uses "you" to address viewers directly
- Conversational markers: "Here's the thing..." / "What's really powerful..." / "The beauty of this is..."

---

## Constructions to avoid

**Em dashes** — use semicolons or start a new sentence instead.

**"Enables" / "enable"** — rewrite. "Helps you do X" or "you can do X" instead.

**Corporate buzzwords:** robust, seamless, cutting-edge, leverage, utilize, delve, myriad, empower (as a standalone verb without a real object), game-changer (unless in direct SME voice), transformative

**Temporal clichés:** "In today's landscape," "in today's fast-moving world," "now more than ever"

**Passive voice:** "X can be configured" → "you can configure X"

**Contrastive negation:** "It's not just X, it's Y" — reads as formulaic

**Filler affirmations in Thomas's responses:** "Excellent advice" — cut or rephrase naturally

**Salesy framing:** avoid phrasing that sounds like a product pitch rather than a colleague conversation

---

## Formatting specifics

- Speaker names in brackets: `[Thomas]` / `[Guest Name]`
- Bullet points only in dialogue — never numbered lists under a speaker tag
- Visual aid callouts inline: `[visual aid: slide]` / `[OVERLAY url]` / `[LOWER THIRD]`
- Multi-speaker staging: `[2up]` / `[3UP]`
- URLs spoken aloud: "aka dot ms forward slash LinkName"
- Microsoft product and feature names in sentence case: "Cloud Accelerate Factory" (not all caps)
- Preserve exact heading text from briefs and outlines; do not rephrase unless explicitly asked
