# AI writing: word and phrase replacement list for RDC writers

A working reference for editing AI-assisted drafts. Use this during your editorial pass. The goal isn't to purge every word on this list from every piece — it's to catch the combinations and densities that make writing sound generated rather than written.

---

## How to use this list

**Tier 1 — Replace on sight.** These signal AI authorship reliably, in any context, for any client. Swap them out every time.

**Tier 2 — Flag when two or more appear in the same paragraph.** Each word is fine alone. When they cluster, the paragraph probably needs a rewrite, not just word swaps.

**Tier 3 — Flag only when the piece is saturated with them.** Common words AI simply overuses. If they show up three or four times in a short section, replace some with specifics: a number, a name, an example.

These are normal words that AI overuses. Only flag when they're showing up three or four times in a short section.

## Phrases to cut or rewrite

### Opener phrases — cut entirely or replace with the point

These are how AI warms up before saying anything. Delete them and start with the actual content.

- "In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape…"
- "In an era where [X]…"
- "In the realm of…"
- "In today's fast-paced world…"
- "In today's digital age…"
- "In the vast [X]…"
- "In this article, we will explore…"
- "Join us as we…"
- "Let's explore…" / "Let's deep dive…" / "Let's break this down"
- "At its core, this is about…"

### Significance inflation — delete or replace with specifics

These phrases treat routine things as historic. If deleting the phrase leaves the sentence intact, delete it.

- "A testament to…" → shows, proves, demonstrates
- "Cannot be overstated" → state it once, clearly
- "Plays a crucial role" → say what the role is
- "Has emerged as a…" → describe what it is now
- "A tapestry of…" → describe the actual thing
- "Stands as a beacon of…" → rewrite entirely
- "Left an indelible mark" → say what the mark is
- "Marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of…" → state what happened

### Structural filler — cut or rewrite as a real connection

- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" → restructure the connection, or use "and," "also"
- "In conclusion" / "In summary" / "To summarize" → your ending should be obvious without announcing it
- "It's worth noting that" / "Notably" → just state the fact
- "That being said" → use "but" or "however" and don't overuse either
- "When it comes to…" → just talk about the thing
- "At the end of the day" → cut
- "Here's what's interesting…" → let the content signal its own importance

### Confidence calibration — let the fact speak

- "Interestingly" / "Surprisingly" / "Importantly" / "Significantly" / "Notably" → cut or restructure
- "It's important to note that" → cut; state the fact
- "Needless to say" → cut

### Chatbot residue — remove entirely

These belong in a chat window, not a published piece.

- "I hope this helps!"
- "Certainly!" / "Absolutely!" / "Great question!" / "Excellent point!"
- "Feel free to reach out"
- "Let me know if you need anything else"
- "You're asking about…" / "To answer your question…"
- "Let's dive in!" (as a section opener)

### Vague attribution — name the source or cut

When AI can't name a source, it gestures at one. Replace with a real citation or reframe as a general claim.

- "Experts believe…" → [name] argued in [source] that…
- "Studies show…" → [study name, year] found that…
- "Research suggests…" → [organization]'s [year] research found…
- "Industry leaders agree…" → (cut, or name a specific person)

### Generic close — cut or replace with something specific

- "The future looks bright"
- "Only time will tell"
- "One thing is certain"
- "As we move forward"
- "This is just the beginning"

### Promotional language — replace with plain description

- "Nestled within the breathtaking…" → "located in"
- "A vibrant hub of innovation" → describe what makes it active
- "A thriving ecosystem" → describe the actual community, or cite a number

## The patterns that are harder to spot

### The **Bold term: explanation** list

The single most recognizable AI structural pattern. It looks like this:

> **Scalability:** The platform scales automatically based on workload demand.  
> **Security:** Built-in encryption protects data at rest and in transit.  
> **Performance:** Query speeds improve as data volume increases.

Every item starts with a bolded label that duplicates what the sentence says. Rewrite as prose, or use genuine bullet points without the bold-label format.

### The false contrast

"It's not just X — it's Y." "This isn't about X. It's about Y." "No X. No Y. Just Z."

These mimic the shape of an insight without containing one. Usually the X and Y say the same thing at different levels of abstraction. Rewrite as a direct positive statement.

### Uniform paragraph length

If every paragraph in a draft is three to five sentences and roughly the same size, the rhythm is metronomic. Break it deliberately. Some paragraphs should be one sentence. Some should be longer. The variation is what makes prose feel written rather than generated.

### Synonym cycling

AI avoids repeating a word by rotating through synonyms: "developers… engineers… practitioners… builders" in the same paragraph. Human writers repeat the clearest word. Forced variation reads as thesaurus abuse.

## Client-specific notes

**Microsoft content:** Audiences skew technical. Vague attributions ("experts believe") and inflated adjectives ("groundbreaking," "revolutionary") erode credibility fast with this readership. Microsoft has its own style guide — when writing for Microsoft platforms, verify terminology against Microsoft Docs rather than trusting AI definitions.

**Databricks content:** Data and AI practitioners are the primary audience. They have low tolerance for imprecision. When AI generates a technical claim, verify it against Databricks' own documentation. "Leverages a robust ecosystem of integrations" tells a data engineer nothing — name the integrations.

**City of Seattle content:** Audiences range widely — from internal staff to public residents. Clarity matters more than sophistication. AI tends toward elevated register; push back toward plain language. "Utilize" → "use." "Facilitate" → "help." Avoid jargon about the tech stack unless the specific audience is technical.

**General tech clients:** Watch for the innovation inflation pattern — AI positions everything as unprecedented and transformative. Tech content that oversells tends to read as marketing rather than information. Understatement often lands better.
