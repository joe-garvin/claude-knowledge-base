# Christina editorial guidelines — Microsoft SMB AI infographic

**Date:** 2026-03-19
**Source:** Christina Tsai's comments on outline v1
**Folder:** projects

## Summary

These guidelines are derived from Christina's first round of editorial feedback on the SMB AI infographic outline v1. They reflect her preferences and sensitivities as a client and should be consulted before each subsequent drafting round on this project.

---

## Guidelines

**On audience sizing and inclusivity**
Never characterize the audience as small, even implicitly. The SMB range for this project extends to teams of up to 1,000 people. Language like "small team" or any phrase that implies limited headcount will alienate part of the audience. Always anchor on language that works across the full 1–500 employee range.

**On claims and evidence**
Don't use superlatives or ranking language ("most common," "top," "best") unless there is explicit data to support them. Christina is attentive to this and will push back. If something is common or widely applicable, say it's common — but don't claim it's the most common without proof.

**On sequential vs. parallel framing**
Avoid language that implies a prescribed order or progression when the content is actually a set of independent options. "Starting points" implies step one leads to step two. "Applications" or "ways businesses are using AI" is more accurate and doesn't create a false hierarchy.

**On technical language and jargon**
Any phrase that is not immediately clear to a non-technical business owner needs to be simplified or cut. "The data staying yours" failed this test — it assumes familiarity with data sovereignty concepts. When referencing Microsoft Azure's role, keep it simple: Azure keeps your data secure and private.

**On analog vs. digital language**
Be mindful of words that carry a physical or old-fashioned connotation in a digital context. "Paperwork" implies physical paper and filing cabinets. Use "documents," "invoices," "contracts" — terms that apply equally to digital and physical workflows.

**On vague closing phrases**
Every phrase in the copy needs to mean something specific to the reader. "One team," "actually need a human," and similar constructions are too abstract on their own. Always follow a vague phrase with a concrete example or clarifying detail — or replace it with something specific from the start.

**On Microsoft Azure mentions**
Christina's suggested rewrite of the Azure sentence points to a clear preference: Azure's role should be expressed in terms of what it does for the business (keeps knowledge secure, keeps data private) rather than in technical infrastructure language (underlying infrastructure, data sovereignty). Lead with the business benefit, not the technical capability.

**On trailing clauses that don't add meaning**
Christina flagged "and what businesses are seeing when they get there" as unclear and suggested removing it. Watch for phrases that sound like they're adding information but are actually vague — if a clause can be removed without losing meaning, it should be.
