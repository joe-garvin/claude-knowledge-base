### Use case copy (v1 outline — all four sections per use case)

**Chat with your data**
- Business problem: Every growing business runs on knowledge — past proposals, product details, customer history, internal processes. But that knowledge is usually scattered: buried in email threads, saved across shared drives, or stored in someone's head. When your team needs an answer, they spend time hunting for it instead of acting on it. And when someone leaves, they often take critical knowledge with them.
- What changed: AI can connect to the documents and systems a business already has — files, emails, records — and let anyone on the team ask questions in plain language and get accurate, sourced answers in seconds. No more hunting through folders or waiting for the one person who knows. Microsoft Azure provides the underlying infrastructure that makes this work securely, with the data staying yours.
- Business outcome: Businesses using this approach are spending less time searching for information and more time acting on it. Decisions that used to wait on the right person or the right document are getting made faster. Teams are working from the same information instead of different versions of it, which means fewer errors and less back-and-forth. [TBD — flagged to Christina that no customer story maps to this use case]
- Closing line: The starting point is simpler than most businesses expect — because AI works with the information you already have. You don't need to reorganize your files or rebuild your systems. Whether your knowledge lives in a handful of shared documents or years of accumulated records, getting started doesn't require starting over.

**Customer service**
- Business problem: Customers expect fast, helpful responses — but most growing businesses can't staff for that around the clock. Questions come in after hours, on weekends, and faster than a small team can handle. Every slow response or missed follow-up is a potential customer lost.
- What changed: AI handles routine and complex customer questions automatically, any time of day, so customers get faster answers and staff can focus on the interactions that actually need a human. It works alongside your existing support process rather than replacing it — and Microsoft Azure provides the security and reliability to run it at scale.
- Business outcome: For one auto dealership platform, the results were measurable. CallRevu — which helps dealerships analyze customer calls and automate follow-up — saw a 15% lift in lead conversion, 20% fewer missed opportunities, and 10% higher customer satisfaction across its network, with staff spending less time sorting through incoming requests and more time on the conversations that matter.
- Closing line: Better customer service doesn't always mean more staff. For businesses of every size, AI support means customers get answers faster — and teams get time back.

**Document intelligence**
- Business problem: For most growing businesses, paperwork never stops — invoices, contracts, intake forms, inspection reports. Someone has to read each one, pull out the relevant information, and enter it somewhere else. It's time-consuming, error-prone, and one of the hardest things to scale as a business grows.
- What changed: AI reads documents and extracts the information that matters — automatically. Invoices get reconciled, contract renewal dates get flagged, intake forms get processed — without anyone having to do it by hand. Microsoft Azure handles the extraction securely, so the focus stays on exceptions rather than data entry.
- Business outcome: For one legal software platform, the impact was significant. Assembly Software's NeosAI — built to automate document workflows for law firms — saved firms up to 25 hours per case on manual data entry and document review, and cut document drafting time from 40 hours to minutes.
- Closing line: For any business that runs on paperwork — and most do — this is time waiting to be recovered. Nothing about the existing process needs to change. AI works with documents exactly as they come in, so nothing about the way work gets done has to change.

## Key decisions and principles established this session

- All six PMM customer stories are vendor/ISV stories, not SMB end-user stories — this is typical of Microsoft's library; the right filter is whether outcomes describe what end customers experienced, not whether the story is told from an SMB's perspective
- "Business problem," "What changed," "Business outcome," and "Closing line" are internal working labels — not intended as on-page headers
- "Use case 1/2/3" are internal organizational labels — "Chat with your data," "Customer service," and "Document intelligence" are the proposed on-page display names
- Businesses (not AI) should be the grammatical subject/protagonist in copy wherever possible
- Avoid repeating "growing businesses" across headline and body copy — use "businesses of every size" as alternate
- Comments in the outline serve as the primary vehicle for flagging open questions to Christina — keeps the outline clean and the asks visible in context
- The outline is a client-facing document, not an internal strategy document — language, labels, and comments should all reflect that

## Language principles reinforced this session
- No "beat," "framework," "treatment," "outcome beat," or other insider content strategy terminology in client-facing or PM-facing copy
- No collective "we" — first-person singular or passive voice
- No contrastive negation constructions
- Plain, conversational, toned-down language throughout
- Sentence case for all titles, headings, and subheadings
