# Ops, HR, and leadership — interview guide

## Role context

This covers Brian (ops/HR) and Chris (managing partner), who handle the operational and business side of RDC: contracts, SOWs, proposals, HR documentation, onboarding, client relationship management, business development, and internal process design.

Common work patterns:
- Drafting and adapting legal and business documents (SOWs, change orders, proposals, RFP responses)
- Managing HR processes: job descriptions, onboarding materials, policy documentation
- Client communication at the relationship and business level
- Business development: identifying opportunities, crafting pitches or introductions
- Internal communication: team announcements, policy updates, process documentation

High-value AI opportunities in this role tend to cluster around document generation from templates, research synthesis, and communication drafting.

## Likely friction areas to probe

- **SOW and proposal generation**: Building client-facing business documents from existing templates and project details — high repetition, high stakes
- **HR document generation**: Job descriptions, onboarding docs, policy write-ups that follow predictable structures
- **RFP research and response**: Finding relevant RFPs and drafting competitive responses
- **Client communication**: Drafting business-level correspondence that's professional and appropriately calibrated
- **Internal process documentation**: Writing up processes, standards, or guidelines for internal use
- **Contract or document review**: Reading through contracts or vendor documents to extract key terms, obligations, or flags

## Question sequence

After role is confirmed, continue:

**Q2: Work focus**
> "Where does most of your working time go?"

Options:
- Business documents — proposals, SOWs, contracts
- Client relationships and communication
- HR and operations — hiring, onboarding, policy
- Internal process and team management
- Something else

**Q3: Document generation**
> "When you need to build a business document — a proposal, SOW, change order — what's the most time-consuming part?"

Options:
- Adapting the template to fit the specific project
- Getting the right level of detail in the scope and deliverables
- Making sure the language is professional and tight
- Going back and forth on wording and specifics
- Something else

**Q4: Friction drill-down**

If they said "adapting templates":
> "What makes template adaptation slow?"

Options:
- The details from the brief don't map cleanly to the template sections
- I have to hunt through past documents to find the right boilerplate language
- Each client has slightly different conventions I have to remember
- Getting the pricing and scope tables right
- Something else

**Q5: Communication volume**
> "How much of your day involves writing — emails, proposals, internal docs?"

Options:
- It's most of what I do
- A significant chunk — maybe half
- It comes and goes
- Less than you'd think

**Q6: AI experience**
> "Have you used Claude or any AI tools for work before?"

Options:
- Yes, fairly regularly
- A few times, with mixed results
- Once or twice but it didn't really stick
- Not really

**Q7: Barrier check**
> "What would make an AI solution feel too risky or too complicated?"

Options:
- Concern about confidentiality — what goes into the AI
- If the output needs so much editing it's not worth it
- If it only handles simple tasks and misses the nuance in legal/business docs
- If it requires significant setup time
- Something else

## Output guidance for ops and leadership

Strong recommendation candidates for this role:
- An SOW draft generator: paste project details from the brief or email thread, get a first-pass SOW draft in the right structure to refine
- A change order generator: describe what changed in scope, get a formatted change order ready to send
- An HR document generator: paste a role description or requirements, get a job description or onboarding doc draft
- A document review / key terms extractor: paste a contract or vendor document, get a plain-language summary of key obligations, dates, and flags
- A proposal or pitch draft: describe the client, the opportunity, and RDC's relevant experience, get a first-pass proposal narrative

Note: be aware of confidentiality instincts here. Acknowledge if the person expresses concern about putting sensitive business information into an AI tool — that's a legitimate concern and worth addressing honestly. Claude.ai does not store or train on conversation data, but the decision about what to put in is always theirs.
