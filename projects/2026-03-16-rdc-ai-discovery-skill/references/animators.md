# Motion graphics artists and animators — interview guide

## Role context

Motion graphics artists and animators at RDC produce 2D and 2.5D animated content: animated social videos, motion graphics for explainer videos, animatics, storyboards, and visual treatments for video projects. Work typically starts from a brief, a script, or a storyboard, and involves interpreting visual direction and bringing it to motion.

Common work patterns:
- Receiving a script or brief and building an animatic or storyboard before full production
- Working through multiple rounds of feedback on motion, timing, and visual treatment
- Adapting existing motion templates or past work to new projects
- Coordinating with writers, designers, and video editors on combined deliverables
- Managing asset organization across projects

AI's direct role in creating motion graphics is very limited. The strongest opportunities are in the communication, organizational, and brief-interpretation side of the work — not in the animation itself.

## Likely friction areas to probe

- **Brief interpretation**: Receiving a written brief that isn't specific about motion direction, style, or feeling — having to make significant creative decisions based on limited guidance
- **Storyboard descriptions**: Writing text descriptions of visual sequences for storyboard approval or client review
- **Feedback interpretation**: Getting feedback on motion work that's vague ("make it feel faster", "more energy") and trying to understand what's actually being asked
- **Version notes**: Tracking what changed between rounds of a motion project
- **Client/PM communication**: Explaining technical constraints or production considerations to non-technical stakeholders
- **Reference research**: Finding motion reference examples, style references, or visual inspiration for a new brief

## Question sequence

After role is confirmed, continue:

**Q2: Work mix**
> "What takes up most of your time in a typical project?"

Options:
- The actual animation and motion work
- Storyboarding or animatics before production
- Feedback rounds and revisions
- Client or PM communication
- Something else

**Q3: Where friction lives**
> "What part of the process do you find most tedious or frustrating?"

Options:
- Getting a brief that doesn't give me enough direction
- Interpreting vague feedback and figuring out what to actually change
- Writing things up — storyboard notes, version summaries, handoff docs
- Managing assets and file organization
- Something else

**Q4: Drill-down**

If they said "vague briefs":
> "When a brief doesn't give you enough visual direction, what do you usually do?"

Options:
- Ask questions and wait — which delays things
- Make my best interpretation and present options
- Search for reference examples on my own
- Start something and use the first round of feedback to calibrate
- Something else

If they said "vague feedback":
> "When feedback on motion work is vague, what's hardest about it?"

Options:
- Figuring out what they actually want from "make it feel more energetic"
- When the feedback contradicts what the brief originally said
- When multiple people give conflicting feedback
- Explaining to stakeholders why certain things aren't technically feasible
- Something else

If they said "writing things up":
> "What written tasks come up most in your work?"

Options:
- Describing visual sequences for storyboard approval
- Version notes — what changed in this round
- Handoff notes for editors or other team members
- Responding to client feedback in writing
- Something else

**Q5: AI experience**
> "Have you used Claude or any AI tools for work before?"

Options:
- Yes, fairly regularly
- A few times, with mixed results
- Once or twice
- Not really

**Q6: Barrier check**
> "What would make an AI tool feel not worth using for your work?"

Options:
- If it can't actually help with the visual/motion side of things
- If it requires a lot of writing or setup from me
- If the output needs too much editing
- I'm open — tell me what's realistic
- Something else

## Output guidance for animators

Strong recommendation candidates for this role:
- A brief-to-questions workflow: paste a brief and ask Claude to identify what's unclear or underspecified before you start, generating a list of questions for the PM
- A vague-feedback translator: paste the feedback you received, describe what you think it means, and ask Claude to help you articulate a response or plan of action
- A storyboard description writer: describe a sequence verbally or in rough notes, get polished storyboard direction text for client approval
- A version summary generator: paste notes on what changed, get a clean round summary

Be honest in the output about the limits of AI for motion work — don't promise things that aren't realistic. The value is in the written and communication-adjacent tasks, and the output should reflect that clearly.
