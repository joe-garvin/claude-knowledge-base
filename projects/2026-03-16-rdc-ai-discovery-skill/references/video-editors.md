# Video editors — interview guide

## Role context

Video editors at RDC (Jeffrey Griffith) work primarily on Azure Essentials Show episodes and other client video projects. Work involves editing recorded footage, applying graphics and lower thirds, syncing audio, managing review cuts, and producing final deliverables. For RDC's in-house Open Door video series, the scope may extend to more creative editorial involvement.

Common work patterns:
- Receiving raw footage and a script or rough cut brief and building an edit
- Going through multiple review rounds with clients or internal stakeholders
- Coordinating with motion graphics artists on graphic inserts and lower thirds
- Managing large files and project assets across versions

AI's direct role in video editing is limited. The strongest opportunities are in the written and communication-adjacent side of the work.

## Likely friction areas to probe

- **Brief interpretation**: Receiving direction that's light on specifics about pacing, tone, or edit style
- **Review notes**: Writing time-coded feedback summaries or change descriptions for client review rounds
- **Client communication**: Drafting responses to client feedback, especially explaining editorial decisions
- **Version tracking**: Keeping clear records of what changed between cuts
- **Script or transcript alignment**: Comparing a script to a recorded transcript before editing

## Question sequence

**Q3 — Project mix** (buttons):
> "What kind of video editing work takes up most of your time?"

Options: Episode editing (Azure Essentials Show or similar) / Short-form social clips from longer content / Instructional or how-to videos / A mix — it varies a lot / Something else

**Q4 — Where friction lives** (buttons):
> "What part of the editing process takes longer than it should?"

Options: Getting clear enough direction before I start / Managing review rounds and keeping track of changes / Writing things up — cut notes, version descriptions, handoff docs / Coordinating with other team members on assets or graphics / Something else

**Q5 — Drill-down** (buttons, branch on Q4 answer):

If "review rounds and change tracking":
> "What's most annoying about managing review rounds?"
Options: Feedback comes in from multiple places and isn't organized / Time codes in feedback don't always match the cut I sent / Tracking what was addressed and what's still outstanding / Writing up what changed between rounds / Something else

If "getting clear direction":
> "When direction is vague, what do you usually do?"
Options: Make my best interpretation and see what feedback comes back / Ask questions upfront, which sometimes delays things / Look at past similar projects as a reference / Start conservative and offer options / Something else

**Q7 — AI experience** (buttons):

Options: Yes, fairly regularly / A few times, with mixed results / Once or twice / Not really

**Q8 — Barrier check** (buttons):
> "What would make an AI tool feel not worth using in your work?"

Options: If it doesn't work with my actual editing software / If it requires a lot of writing or setup from me / If the output still needs major editing / I'm open — tell me what's realistic / Something else

## Output guidance

Strong recommendation candidates for this role:
- A change summary generator: paste rough notes on what changed between cuts, get a clean version summary for the client
- A feedback organizer: paste unstructured review feedback, get a time-coded action list organized for editing
- A brief-to-questions workflow: paste a vague edit brief, get clarifying questions to ask before starting
- A client communication draft: describe the situation, get a draft response to send

Be realistic about AI's role in the actual editing workflow. Focus the recommendations on written and organizational tasks.
