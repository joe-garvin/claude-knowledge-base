# Visual designers — interview guide

## Role context

Visual designers at RDC produce a wide range of designed assets: slide decks (L200, battlecards, executive, kickoff), infographics, ebooks, social cards, flyers, banners, brochures, and occasionally brand assets. Most work starts from a client brief or content document and involves translating written content into a visual layout — often working within established brand guidelines or adapting past examples as a starting point.

Common work patterns:
- Receiving a content document or brief and building a visual layout from it
- Working from past examples ("make it look like this one, but updated")
- Going through multiple review rounds where feedback is often about copy changes, not just design
- Managing version control across rounds (v1, v2, v3 of a file)
- Writing design notes or alt-text descriptions for production handoff
- Interpreting vague or incomplete briefs and making judgment calls

Design at RDC is highly visual and the primary output is files — decks, PDFs, layered design files. AI's direct role in creating visual assets is limited, but there are real opportunities in the written and organizational side of design work.

## Likely friction areas to probe

- **Brief interpretation**: Getting a vague or incomplete brief and having to figure out what's actually being asked before you can design anything
- **Copy changes during review**: Clients frequently change copy in review rounds — the design is done but now there are text edits scattered across feedback
- **Writing alt-text or design notes**: Producing written descriptions of visual elements for production, accessibility, or handoff — repetitive and time-consuming
- **Version summaries**: Writing up what changed between design rounds — what was updated, why, what was kept
- **Responding to feedback**: Interpreting and organizing design feedback that sometimes contradicts itself or is unclear
- **Research for visual reference**: Finding reference examples, understanding what the client's brand has done before, getting context on the subject matter before designing
- **Deck content suggestions**: When writing placeholder copy or section headers in a deck before the writer has provided final copy

## Question sequence

After role is confirmed, continue:

**Q2: Deliverable mix**
> "What types of design work do you spend the most time on?"

Options:
- Slide decks and presentations
- Infographics and visual documents
- Social media assets
- Ebooks or long-form designed documents
- Something else

**Q3: Where time gets lost**
> "Which part of a typical design project takes longer than it should?"

Options:
- Figuring out what the brief is actually asking before I can start
- Going back and forth on copy changes during review
- Managing feedback — organizing it, figuring out what to act on
- Writing things up: version notes, design handoff docs, alt text
- Something else

**Q4: Drill-down on top friction**

If they said "brief interpretation":
> "When a brief is vague or incomplete, what do you usually do?"

Options:
- Ask the PM to get more info from the client
- Make my best judgment and flag assumptions in my work
- Look at past similar projects and adapt
- Start with something and wait for feedback to correct course
- Something else

If they said "copy changes during review":
> "When copy edits come in during a review round, what's the most annoying part?"

Options:
- There are too many small edits scattered across many slides
- The feedback isn't specific enough to know exactly what to change
- I have to reformat or reflow the design every time copy changes
- Tracking what was changed and confirming it was addressed
- Something else

If they said "managing feedback":
> "What makes design feedback hard to work with?"

Options:
- It's vague — "make it feel more premium" without specifics
- It contradicts itself — different reviewers want different things
- There's a lot of it and it's hard to organize and prioritize
- It arrives in different places — email, comments, calls, Teams messages
- Something else

If they said "writing things up":
> "What written tasks come up most often in your design work?"

Options:
- Alt text or accessibility descriptions
- Version summary notes (what changed in v2 vs v1)
- Design handoff notes for production
- Responding to client feedback in writing
- Something else

**Q5: Content and copy involvement**
> "How much do you interact with the written content in your projects?"

Options:
- A lot — I often have to figure out what copy should say before the writer does
- Somewhat — I get copy but frequently have to reformat or trim it to fit
- Mostly the layout — copy comes to me ready to place
- It varies a lot by project

**Q6: AI experience**
> "Have you used Claude or any AI tools for work before?"

Options:
- Yes, fairly regularly
- A few times, with mixed results
- Once or twice but it didn't really stick
- Not really

**Q7: Barrier check**
> "What would make an AI solution feel too complicated to bother with?"

Options:
- If it doesn't integrate with my design tools (Figma, PowerPoint, etc.)
- If it requires a lot of setup
- If the output still needs major cleanup
- If it only helps with writing, not actual design
- Something else

## Output guidance for visual designers

Strong recommendation candidates for this role:
- A feedback consolidation workflow: paste all the feedback from a review round (copied from email, Teams, etc.), get an organized list of actions to take, grouped by slide or section
- A version summary generator: describe what changed in this round, get a clean version summary to send to the PM or client
- A brief-to-questions workflow: paste a vague brief, get a list of clarifying questions to ask the PM before starting
- A copy-fit helper: paste a block of copy that's too long for a design space, give Claude the constraint (word count, character limit, tone), get a trimmed version
- An alt-text generator: paste descriptions of visual elements or screenshots, get accessibility-ready alt text

For the "first step" in each recommendation, give them an actual example prompt. For example:

> "Here's how to start with feedback consolidation: paste all your review feedback into Claude and say 'I'm a designer working on a slide deck. Here's all the feedback I received. Organize this into a clear action list, grouped by slide number, so I can work through it efficiently.'"

Be realistic about what AI can and can't do in design contexts. Do not oversell. Focus on the written, organizational, and communication-adjacent tasks where AI clearly adds value.
