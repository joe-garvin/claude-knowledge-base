AI-curious but cautious — aware of hype, worried about making the wrong investment, looking for proof that this is real and achievable for a business like theirs.

## Use case writing structure

Every use case follows four sections. These are working labels for drafting — not intended as on-page headers:

1. **Business problem** — plain language, the way a business owner would say it. Creates "that's us" recognition.
2. **What changed** — how AI helped, in terms of what it does. Mention Azure/Microsoft naturally as the trusted enabler. No product feature lists.
3. **Business outcome** — specific, tangible result. Numbers when available. Always in business terms (time, money, satisfaction, error reduction), never technical terms. Each use case has a named customer story to anchor this section (see below).
4. **Closing line** — 1–2 sentences bridging the specific story to the reader's situation. Makes it feel achievable for a business of any size.

## Language rules (confirmed applicable by Christina)

**Use instead of:**
- "SMB" / "small business" → "growing businesses" / "businesses of all sizes" (in titles and headlines especially)
- "organization" → "business" / "company"
- "IT estate" / "data estate" → "technology" / "existing systems"
- Abstract "innovate" / "innovation" / "competitive edge" → tie to tangible outcomes: automation, time savings, cost reduction

**Hard no:**
- "Frontier Firm," "AI-first," "intelligent enterprise," "digital transformation" as standalone phrases
- AI language without tangible business outcome attached

**Additional language rules from Joe's preferences:**
- Sentence case for all titles, headings, subheadings — never title case
- No contrastive negation ("it's not X, it's Y")
- No line dividers
- Plain, conversational, toned-down language — not polished or flowery
- No collective "we" — first-person singular or passive voice
- No insider content strategy terminology: not "beat," "framework," "treatment," "outcome beat," "content module"
- Businesses (not AI, not technology) should be the grammatical subject wherever possible
- Avoid repeating the same noun phrase across headline and body copy

## Christina's editorial guidelines (from outline v1 feedback)

These reflect Christina's specific preferences and sensitivities. Apply to every drafting round.

**On audience sizing and inclusivity**
Never characterize the audience as small, even implicitly. Always anchor on language that works across the full 1–500 employee range.

**On claims and evidence**
Don't use superlatives or ranking language ("most common," "top," "best") unless there is explicit data to support them. If something is common or widely applicable, say it's common — but don't claim it's the most common without proof.

**On sequential vs. parallel framing**
Avoid language that implies a prescribed order or progression when the content is actually a set of independent options.

**On technical language and jargon**
Any phrase that is not immediately clear to a non-technical business owner needs to be simplified or cut. When referencing Microsoft Azure's role, keep it simple: Azure keeps your data secure and private.

**On analog vs. digital language**
Be mindful of words that carry a physical or old-fashioned connotation in a digital context. "Paperwork" implies physical paper and filing cabinets. Use "documents," "invoices," "contracts."

**On vague closing phrases**
Every phrase needs to mean something specific to the reader. Always follow a vague phrase with a concrete example or clarifying detail.

**On Microsoft Azure mentions**
Express Azure's role in terms of what it does for the business (keeps knowledge secure, keeps data private) rather than in technical infrastructure language. Lead with the business benefit, not the technical capability.

**On trailing clauses that don't add meaning**
If a clause can be removed without losing meaning, remove it.

## Approved outline content (v1, incorporating Christina's feedback)

### Page 1

**Opening section**
- Headline: How growing businesses are putting AI to work
- Intro line A: Businesses of every size are saving time, serving customers better, and making smarter decisions with AI — in ways that are more practical and accessible than most expect.
- Intro line B: Here are three of the ways businesses are applying AI right now.

**Use case 1: Chat with your data**

Business problem:
Every growing business runs on knowledge — past proposals, product details, customer history, internal processes. But that knowledge is usually scattered: buried in email threads, saved across shared drives, or stored in someone's head. When your team needs an answer, they spend time hunting for it instead of acting on it. And when someone leaves, they often take critical knowledge with them.

What changed:
AI can connect to the documents and systems a business already has — files, emails, records — and let anyone on the team ask questions in plain language and get accurate, sourced answers in seconds. No more hunting through folders or waiting for the one person who knows. Microsoft Azure handles the security, so your business data stays private and accessible only to your team.

Business outcome:
For one staffing platform, the results were measurable. Gojob — which helps match temporary workers with employers — used AI to let recruiters query a database of two million candidate profiles in plain language, surfacing the right match in minutes rather than days. Their placement rate rose from 60% to 95%, and recruiters went from contacting 50 to 80 candidates per vacancy down to fewer than two.

Closing line:
The starting point is simpler than most businesses expect — because AI works with the information you already have. You don't need to reorganize your files or rebuild your systems. Whether your knowledge lives in a handful of shared documents or years of accumulated records, getting started doesn't require starting over.

### Page 2

**Use case 2: Customer service**

Business problem:
Customers expect fast, helpful responses — but most growing businesses can't staff for that around the clock. Questions come in after hours, on weekends, and faster than staff alone can handle. Every slow response or missed follow-up is a potential customer lost.

What changed:
AI handles routine and complex customer questions automatically, any time of day, so customers get faster answers and staff can focus on complex inquiries, sensitive situations, and relationships that benefit from a human touch. It works alongside your existing support process rather than replacing it — and Microsoft Azure provides the security and reliability to run it at scale.

Business outcome:
For one auto dealership platform, the results were measurable. CallRevu — which helps dealerships analyze customer calls and automate follow-up — saw a 15% lift in lead conversion, 20% fewer missed opportunities, and 10% higher customer satisfaction across its network, with staff spending less time sorting through incoming requests and more time on the conversations that matter.

Closing line:
Better customer service doesn't always mean more staff. For businesses of every size, AI support means customers get answers faster — and teams get time back.

**Use case 3: Document intelligence**

Business problem:
For most growing businesses, documents never stop arriving — invoices, contracts, intake forms, inspection reports. Someone has to read each one, pull out the relevant information, and enter it somewhere else. It's time-consuming, error-prone, and one of the hardest things to scale as a business grows.

What changed:
AI reads documents and extracts the information that matters — automatically. Invoices get reconciled, contract renewal dates get flagged, intake forms get processed — without anyone having to do it by hand. Microsoft Azure handles the extraction securely, so the focus stays on exceptions rather than data entry.

Business outcome:
For one legal software platform, the impact was significant. Assembly Software's NeosAI — built to automate document workflows for law firms — saved firms up to 25 hours per case on manual data entry and document review and cut document drafting time from 40 hours to minutes.

Closing line:
For any business managing a constant stream of documents — and most do — this is time waiting to be recovered. Nothing about the existing process needs to change. AI works with documents exactly as they come in, so nothing about the way work gets done has to change.

---

### Page 3

**Use case 4: Security and fraud detection**

Business problem:
Every business that handles money, customer accounts, or financial transactions is a target for fraud. The challenge isn't just catching fraud when it happens — it's catching it before a payment goes out, a claim gets processed, or a fraudulent account gets opened. Manual review can only cover so much, and as fraud schemes grow more sophisticated, the gap between what human reviewers can catch and what actually gets through keeps widening.

What changed:
AI monitors transactions, claims, and account activity continuously — flagging suspicious patterns in real time so teams can investigate before damage is done. Rather than replacing investigators, it directs their attention to the cases that most need it. Microsoft Azure keeps business data secure and private, with the reliability to run this across operations of any size.

Business outcome:
For one AI-powered insurance platform, the results were measurable. Shift Technology — which helps hundreds of insurers detect claims fraud — used Azure AI Document Intelligence and Azure AI Vision to automatically classify and extract information from documents that previously required weeks of manual sorting and labeling, reducing that process to days. With more than 2.6 billion policies and claims analyzed to date, insurers using the platform can flag suspicious activity faster and make decisions that manual review would miss entirely.

Closing line:
Fraud doesn't announce itself, and no business is too large or too established to be a target. AI gives businesses of every size the ability to monitor more, catch more, and act faster — without adding headcount to do it.

Closing message:
AI doesn't have to mean a big investment or a long runway to results. The businesses seeing the most impact started small — with one process, one problem, one place to start.
